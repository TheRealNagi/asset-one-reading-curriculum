Book for the ½ Month
M½
Reminiscences of a Stock Operator
Edwin Lefèvre — disguised biography of Jesse Livermore
Why this book: The single most widely read book in trading history. It reads as a novel — fast-paced, entertaining, no equations — yet smuggles in every foundational market concept: speculation vs. investing, crowd behavior, why markets move, the emotional rollercoaster of a trading career, and the rare proof that consistent profits are possible. It answers "is this worth doing?" better than any textbook. Pairs perfectly with the intro video: the video explains the system, this book shows a human living inside it.
Instructor note: Pair with chapters from "The Ascent of Money" (also in library) if the group wants a broader financial history angle. Livermore is the hook; Ferguson is the context.

Month 1 — Personal Finance & Wealth Mindset
M1
Rich Dad Poor Dad
Robert T. Kiyosaki
Why this book: The most universally accessible entry point into financial literacy. The asset/liability mental model, the cash flow quadrant, the idea that your house is not an asset — these concepts rewire how students think about money before they touch a single chart. Controversial, yes, and intentionally simplified — but those are features, not bugs, for Level 1. Every student regardless of background walks away with a shifted frame. Do not expect investing rigor; expect a mindset overhaul.
Instructor flag: Kiyosaki over-simplifies and some advice is legally/practically dubious. Frame it explicitly as a mindset primer, not an investing manual. The financial independence philosophy is what matters here.

Month 2 — Trading Psychology & Mental Framework
M2
Trading in the Zone
Mark Douglas
Why this book: The definitive text on trading psychology — arguably the most important book in the entire curriculum. Douglas explains why technically proficient traders still lose: they haven't internalized probability thinking. Concepts like "thinking in distributions," eliminating the need to know what comes next, and building a beliefs system consistent with market realities are foundational to everything that follows. Short enough (240 pages) to read twice in a month. Students should keep this book for life.
Instructor note: Test comprehension on the five fundamental truths and the concept of a "trading edge." Students who internalize this before learning strategy will learn strategy faster.

Month 1 — Macro & Fundamental Analysis
M1
The Trader's Guide to Key Economic Indicators
Richard Yamarone
Why this book: Bridges the gap between abstract economics and real market moves. Covers every major data release — GDP, CPI, NFP, PMI, retail sales — explaining what each measures, why markets react, and how to position around them. Written explicitly for traders, not economists. Mid-level execution: conceptual enough to explain the "why," practical enough to apply immediately in news-based trading. The most directly useful macro reference for a developing trader.

Month 2 — Value & Financial Investing
M2
The Intelligent Investor
Benjamin Graham, revised by Jason Zweig
Why this book: Warren Buffett called it "the best book about investing ever written." The modern revised edition with Zweig's commentary is highly readable, grounding the 1949 principles in contemporary examples. Covers margin of safety, Mr. Market allegory, the difference between investor and speculator, and how to read companies as businesses. This is the conceptual backbone of all fundamental analysis that follows. Not a quick read — 15 hrs means reading the core chapters carefully, not cover to cover.
Instructor note: Focus chapters 1, 8, 20, and the Postscript for the core philosophy. Chapters on specific calculations can be assigned as reference, not primary reading.

Month 3 — Technical Analysis
M3
Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets
John J. Murphy
Why this book: The industry-standard TA textbook — used in the CMT curriculum. Covers everything from Dow Theory and chart construction to trends, support/resistance, patterns, volume, and major indicators. Murphy's writing is clear and visual. This is the single book that earns its place as a permanent reference, not just a one-time read. At Level 2, students work through the conceptual half (Chs. 1–10); at later levels, the indicator chapters become active working references.
Instructor note: Assign Chapters 1–10 for the month. Chapters 11–20 on indicators are reference material for Level 3 onwards. The chapter on intermarket analysis (Ch. 18) is a preview of Level 4.

Month 1 — System Design Philosophy
M1
Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom
Van K. Tharp
Why this book: The best book on how to think about building a trading system before building one. Tharp introduces expectancy (the mathematical edge), position sizing as the prime driver of returns, and the concept that there is no universal system — only systems that fit individual traders. Essential for wiring the mind correctly before picking strategies. Forms the "why systematic?" justification that underlies all of Level 3.

Month 2 — Structured Stock Strategy (Momentum + Research)
M2
How to Make Money in Stocks
William J. O'Neil
Why this book: The CAN SLIM system is one of the few retail-accessible methodologies that combines fundamental screens (earnings, sales, margins) with technical entry rules (breakouts from proper bases) into a complete, testable strategy. O'Neil demonstrates how to go from research idea → chart setup → entry criteria → exit rules — exactly the "from idea to execution" requirement. Concrete, rules-based, and historically back-tested over decades.

Month 3 — Complete Trading Framework
M3
The New Trading for a Living
Alexander Elder
Why this book: Elder's "Three Ms" framework — Mind (psychology), Method (technical strategy), Money (risk management) — is the most coherent trilogy for a developing trader. This updated edition covers triple screen strategy, Elder-Ray, Force Index, and a complete trading plan structure. Mid-level execution: not a pure beginner book, not an advanced one. Integrates everything learned in Levels 1–2 into a working operational system.

Month 4 — Backtesting & System Validation
M4
Building Winning Algorithmic Trading Systems
Kevin J. Davey
Why this book: Written by a three-time World Cup Trading Champion. Davey shows step-by-step how to design a strategy, stress-test it, walk-forward optimize it, and transition it to live trading. Critically, he also explains the ways backtests fail — overfitting, look-ahead bias, selection bias — making students intelligent skeptics of their own systems. The most practical backtesting-to-execution book in the library. Accessible without requiring programming.

Month 4 — Options Strategy (Comprehensive)
M4
Options as a Strategic Investment
Lawrence G. McMillan
Why this book: The professional options reference — exhaustive coverage of every major strategy with risk/reward profiles, Greeks exposure, and tactical adjustments. At 1,000+ pages it is not read cover-to-cover; assign the core strategy chapters (Chs. 1–10, 15, 20) and treat the rest as a lifelong reference. After Thomsett's foundation, this is the book practitioners use.
Instructor flag: This is dense and long. Assign specific chapters, not the full book. Focus: buying and selling puts/calls, spreads, straddles, and the Greeks chapter. Omit exotic structures until Level 5.

Month 5 — Options Pricing & Volatility
M5
Option Volatility and Pricing
Sheldon Natenberg
Why this book: The text that options market makers study. Covers theoretical pricing, the Greeks (delta, gamma, vega, theta, rho) in full practical depth, volatility trading strategies, and risk management. After two months of strategy, students need to understand why options are priced the way they are and how volatility is the true underlying variable being traded. This permanently upgrades options intuition.

Month 7 — Derivatives (Comprehensive)
M7
Options, Futures and Other Derivatives
John C. Hull
Why this book: The academic standard for derivatives — used in every finance MBA program and professional certification. Covers futures pricing, binomial trees, Black-Scholes, swaps, credit derivatives, exotic options. Not every section is accessible to every student, but the conceptual rigor it provides on pricing and risk management is unmatched. Month 9 — Crypto & Digital Assets
M9
The Bitcoin Standard
Saifedean Ammous
Why this book: The strongest first-principles argument for understanding what Bitcoin and crypto actually represent economically — monetary theory, sound money, the history of monetary debasement, and what decentralized currency changes about the financial system. Students who only read crypto trading tactics miss the structural shift crypto represents. This provides the "why does this asset class exist?" foundation that makes crypto more than speculative noise.
Instructor note: Ammous is an Austrian economist and Bitcoin maximalist — his views are intentionally one-sided. Pair with an assigned chapter from "Crypto Uncovered" (also in library) for balance on altcoins.

Month 12 — Level Capstone: Stories from the Masters
M12
Market Wizards

5 PROFESSIONAL
Professional / Advanced — Lifetime Rotation
Market insights, research papers, institutional memos, philosophy, and keeping current — weekly group meeting, 4×/month
Lifetime · Weekly 3-hr group sessions ~12 hrs/month 12-book annual rotation
Why a rotation, not a single pick: Level 5 is not a course with a fixed syllabus — it is a practice. The group's reading should rotate annually through great books, and supplement reading with research papers, institutional letters (Bridgewater Daily Observations, Howard Marks memos, Berkshire letters), and live market analysis. Below is a 12-book annual rotation. Cycle through these, replacing books as members have read them with new selections from the library.
ROTATION 01 — PHILOSOPHY
Poor Charlie's Almanack
Charles T. Munger
Mental models for investing and life. Munger's latticework of models from multiple disciplines is the professional trader's operating system.
ROTATION 02 — FOUNDATION
Security Analysis
Graham & Dodd
The original institutional investment text. Demands slow, careful reading. Professional-level fundamental analysis from first principles.
ROTATION 03 — MACRO THEORY
Big Debt Crises
Ray Dalio
Dalio's template for debt cycles — archetypal crisis analysis with case studies. Essential macro framework for professional portfolio context.
ROTATION 04 — HISTORY
Manias, Panics, and Crashes
Charles P. Kindleberger
The definitive historical study of financial crises across five centuries. Teaches pattern recognition in bubble/crash dynamics.
ROTATION 05 — EDGE
The Alchemy of Finance
George Soros
Soros's theory of reflexivity — how market participants' beliefs alter the fundamentals they reflect. One of the most original ideas in finance.
ROTATION 06 — HEDGE FUNDS
More Money Than God
Sebastian Mallaby
Definitive history of the hedge fund industry — models, strategies, and the personalities who shaped modern markets. Institutional perspective.
ROTATION 07 — FORECASTING
Superforecasting
Philip Tetlock & Dan Gardner
Evidence-based research on prediction. Directly applicable to investment thesis construction, probability estimation, and research rigor.
ROTATION 08 — QUANT ML
Advances in Financial Machine Learning
Marcos López de Prado
The frontier of quantitative research. Dense and technical — for groups with quantitative members. The most cited recent quant finance book.
ROTATION 09 — NARRATIVE
The Man Who Solved the Markets
Gregory Zuckerman
Renaissance Technologies / Jim Simons story. Explores what the most successful quant fund in history actually does, and the culture behind consistent alpha.
ROTATION 10 — MACRO PRACTICE
Global Macro Trading
Greg Gliner
Practitioner-level guide to macro trading across all asset classes. Covers building macro views, expressing them, and managing macro risk at a professional level.
ROTATION 11 — PORTFOLIO
Active Portfolio Management
Grinold & Kahn
Institutional standard for active management — the Fundamental Law of Active Management, information ratios, portfolio construction. Quantitative but transformative.
ROTATION 12 — RISK
Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk
Peter L. Bernstein
A masterpiece on the history of human understanding of risk — from dice to derivatives. Deepens the philosophical relationship every professional has with uncertainty.
Supplementary (non-book) sources for Level 5: Bridgewater's Daily Observations and Principles, Howard Marks memos (Oaktree Capital), Berkshire Hathaway annual letters, NBER working papers, academic journals (Journal of Finance, Journal of Portfolio Management), institutional research from Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and similar. These should form 50–60% of session material; books anchor the conceptual framework.
