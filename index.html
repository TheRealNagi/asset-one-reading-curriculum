Book for the ½ Month
M½
Reminiscences of a Stock Operator
Edwin Lefèvre — disguised biography of Jesse Livermore
Why this book: The single most widely read book in trading history. It reads as a novel — fast-paced, entertaining, no equations — yet smuggles in every foundational market concept: speculation vs. investing, crowd behavior, why markets move, the emotional rollercoaster of a trading career, and the rare proof that consistent profits are possible. It answers "is this worth doing?" better than any textbook. Pairs perfectly with the intro video: the video explains the system, this book shows a human living inside it.
Instructor note: Pair with chapters from "The Ascent of Money" (also in library) if the group wants a broader financial history angle. Livermore is the hook; Ferguson is the context.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • The Ascent of Money — Niall Ferguson breadth — Sweeping financial history of the world: bonds, stocks, insurance, real estate, derivatives. Ideal context layer for the intro video's market-theory content.
  • How an Economy Grows and Why It Crashes — Peter Schiff breadth — Short illustrated fable on economic fundamentals. Extremely accessible; useful if students need economics basics reinforced before Level 2.
  • Jesse Livermore: World's Greatest Stock Trader — Richard Smitten depth — Full biographical treatment of Livermore beyond the Lefèvre account. For students hooked by Reminiscences who want the complete, documented story.

Month 1 — Personal Finance & Wealth Mindset
M1
Rich Dad Poor Dad
Robert T. Kiyosaki
Why this book: The most universally accessible entry point into financial literacy. The asset/liability mental model, the cash flow quadrant, the idea that your house is not an asset — these concepts rewire how students think about money before they touch a single chart. Controversial, yes, and intentionally simplified — but those are features, not bugs, for Level 1. Every student regardless of background walks away with a shifted frame. Do not expect investing rigor; expect a mindset overhaul.
Instructor flag: Kiyosaki over-simplifies and some advice is legally/practically dubious. Frame it explicitly as a mindset primer, not an investing manual. The financial independence philosophy is what matters here.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • The Millionaire Next Door — Thomas Stanley & William Danko depth — Data-driven counterpoint to Kiyosaki. Shows how actual millionaires build wealth — frugality, discipline, income-to-lifestyle ratio. Grounds the mindset in empirical reality.
  • MONEY Master the Game — Tony Robbins breadth — Interviews with 50 top investors distilled into a wealth-building framework. More comprehensive on investment vehicles than Kiyosaki; accessible but substantial.
  • The Barefoot Investor — Scott Pape execution — Extremely practical step-by-step personal finance system: accounts, buckets, debt payoff, insurance. Best "now do this" companion to Kiyosaki's mindset shift.

Month 2 — Trading Psychology & Mental Framework
M2
Trading in the Zone
Mark Douglas
Why this book: The definitive text on trading psychology — arguably the most important book in the entire curriculum. Douglas explains why technically proficient traders still lose: they haven't internalized probability thinking. Concepts like "thinking in distributions," eliminating the need to know what comes next, and building a beliefs system consistent with market realities are foundational to everything that follows. Short enough (240 pages) to read twice in a month. Students should keep this book for life.
Instructor note: Test comprehension on the five fundamental truths and the concept of a "trading edge." Students who internalize this before learning strategy will learn strategy faster.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • The Disciplined Trader — Mark Douglas depth — Douglas's earlier book, which Trading in the Zone grew out of. More philosophical and slower-paced; excellent for students who want to go deeper on the belief-system framework before Layer 3 strategy work.
  • Trading Psychology 2.0 — Brett Steenbarger breadth — The modern, research-backed companion to Douglas. Steenbarger integrates cognitive science, performance psychology, and trading. More evidence-based than Douglas's introspective approach — useful for analytically-minded students.
  • The Daily Trading Coach — Brett Steenbarger execution — 101 structured lessons for self-coaching. Workbook-style; ideal for students who want to apply psychology concepts through daily practice rather than just conceptual reading.

Month 1 — Macro & Fundamental Analysis
M1
The Trader's Guide to Key Economic Indicators
Richard Yamarone
Why this book: Bridges the gap between abstract economics and real market moves. Covers every major data release — GDP, CPI, NFP, PMI, retail sales — explaining what each measures, why markets react, and how to position around them. Written explicitly for traders, not economists. Mid-level execution: conceptual enough to explain the "why," practical enough to apply immediately in news-based trading. The most directly useful macro reference for a developing trader.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • The Secrets of Economic Indicators — Bernard Baumohl breadth — The most comprehensive catalogue of economic indicators — broader coverage than Yamarone's trader-focused treatment. Better as a reference shelf book than a cover-to-cover read.
  • Market Sense and Nonsense — Jack Schwager depth — Dismantles common investment myths (past performance, diversification fallacies) using data. Provides critical thinking armor against the macro narratives students will encounter in trading.
  • Mastering the Market Cycle — Howard Marks depth — How economic and credit cycles shape market returns. Marks's probabilistic, cycle-aware framework is the most durable macro lens for developing traders — complements indicator-reading with broader cycle context.

Month 2 — Value & Financial Investing
M2
The Intelligent Investor
Benjamin Graham, revised by Jason Zweig
Why this book: Warren Buffett called it "the best book about investing ever written." The modern revised edition with Zweig's commentary is highly readable, grounding the 1949 principles in contemporary examples. Covers margin of safety, Mr. Market allegory, the difference between investor and speculator, and how to read companies as businesses. This is the conceptual backbone of all fundamental analysis that follows. Not a quick read — 15 hrs means reading the core chapters carefully, not cover to cover.
Instructor note: Focus chapters 1, 8, 20, and the Postscript for the core philosophy. Chapters on specific calculations can be assigned as reference, not primary reading.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • The Warren Buffett Way — Robert Hagstrom execution — Translates Graham's abstract principles into Buffett's actual investment process. Shows how to apply the Intelligent Investor's philosophy to real stock selection. The most practical companion to Graham for students who want to act on what they've read.
  • Value Investing: From Graham to Buffett and Beyond — Greenwald et al. depth — The Columbia Business School value investing course in book form. Covers franchise value, asset value, and earnings power in modern contexts. More technically rigorous than Graham's prose while remaining accessible.
  • The Most Important Thing Illuminated — Howard Marks breadth — Marks's investment philosophy annotated by peers including Buffett and Klarman. Introduces second-level thinking and risk consciousness as a counterpart to Graham's valuation focus.

Month 3 — Technical Analysis
M3
Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets
John J. Murphy
Why this book: The industry-standard TA textbook — used in the CMT curriculum. Covers everything from Dow Theory and chart construction to trends, support/resistance, patterns, volume, and major indicators. Murphy's writing is clear and visual. This is the single book that earns its place as a permanent reference, not just a one-time read. At Level 2, students work through the conceptual half (Chs. 1–10); at later levels, the indicator chapters become active working references.
Instructor note: Assign Chapters 1–10 for the month. Chapters 11–20 on indicators are reference material for Level 3 onwards. The chapter on intermarket analysis (Ch. 18) is a preview of Level 4.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Technical Analysis: The Complete Resource for Financial Market Technicians — Kirkpatrick & Dahlquist depth — The CMT Program's official companion text. More academically rigorous than Murphy, with statistical testing of TA methods. For students who want the evidence base behind the techniques.
  • Encyclopedia of Chart Patterns — Thomas Bulkowski execution — Statistical performance data for every chart pattern — failure rates, profit targets, breakout statistics. Pure pattern reference; ideal for students focused on execution. One of the most useful trading shelf books available.
  • Evidence Based Technical Analysis — David Aronson depth — Applies rigorous statistical hypothesis testing to TA methods. Challenging but important: forces students to distinguish between patterns with genuine predictive validity and those that are noise. Builds critical thinking about TA claims.

Month 1 — System Design Philosophy
M1
Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom
Van K. Tharp
Why this book: The best book on how to think about building a trading system before building one. Tharp introduces expectancy (the mathematical edge), position sizing as the prime driver of returns, and the concept that there is no universal system — only systems that fit individual traders. Essential for wiring the mind correctly before picking strategies. Forms the "why systematic?" justification that underlies all of Level 3.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Van Tharp's Definitive Guide to Position Sizing — Van K. Tharp depth — The full treatment of Tharp's most important concept. If students absorb one idea from this level, it should be position sizing. This book covers it exhaustively — from fixed fractional to optimal f to portfolio-level sizing.
  • Systematic Trading — Robert Carver depth — A more rigorous, quantitative treatment of building systematic strategies. Carver covers forecast construction, instrument diversification, and capital allocation in a framework compatible with Tharp's philosophy but more mathematically grounded.
  • The New Market Wizards — Jack Schwager breadth — The sequel to Market Wizards. Reading this after Tharp shows students how diverse the approaches of successful systematic traders actually are — validating Tharp's core thesis that there is no single right system.

Month 2 — Structured Stock Strategy (Momentum + Research)
M2
How to Make Money in Stocks
William J. O'Neil
Why this book: The CAN SLIM system is one of the few retail-accessible methodologies that combines fundamental screens (earnings, sales, margins) with technical entry rules (breakouts from proper bases) into a complete, testable strategy. O'Neil demonstrates how to go from research idea → chart setup → entry criteria → exit rules — exactly the "from idea to execution" requirement. Concrete, rules-based, and historically back-tested over decades.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Trade Like an O'Neil Disciple — Gil & Morales execution — Written by two of O'Neil's top analysts. More tactical than O'Neil himself — covers exact entry/exit rules, position sizing in CAN SLIM context, and annotated real trade examples. Strongest execution companion to the primary text.
  • In the Trading Cockpit with the O'Neil Disciples — Gil & Morales execution — The sequel, showing live trading decisions with chart annotations in real-time market conditions. For students who want to see how the system is actually applied under market uncertainty.
  • Momentum Masters — Mark Minervini et al. breadth — Roundtable interviews with four top momentum traders. Broadens perspective beyond CAN SLIM: each practitioner uses momentum slightly differently, showing students how to adapt a framework rather than copy it rigidly.

Month 3 — Complete Trading Framework
M3
The New Trading for a Living
Alexander Elder
Why this book: Elder's "Three Ms" framework — Mind (psychology), Method (technical strategy), Money (risk management) — is the most coherent trilogy for a developing trader. This updated edition covers triple screen strategy, Elder-Ray, Force Index, and a complete trading plan structure. Mid-level execution: not a pure beginner book, not an advanced one. Integrates everything learned in Levels 1–2 into a working operational system.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Come Into My Trading Room — Alexander Elder depth — Elder's earlier, more detailed treatment of the same framework — more comprehensive on the technical side, with fuller discussion of specific indicators. For students who want deeper tactical grounding in Elder's method before backtesting it in Month 4.
  • Entries & Exits — Alexander Elder execution — Elder visits 16 traders' actual trading rooms and annotates their real trades. Bridges the gap between framework and execution by showing how other practitioners implement the Three Ms in practice. Highly concrete.
  • High Performance Trading — Steve Ward breadth — 35 structured strategies from performance psychology applied to trading. Complements Elder's framework with actionable behavioral exercises — useful for students who want to operationalize the psychological components, not just read about them.

Month 4 — Backtesting & System Validation
M4
Building Winning Algorithmic Trading Systems
Kevin J. Davey
Why this book: Written by a three-time World Cup Trading Champion. Davey shows step-by-step how to design a strategy, stress-test it, walk-forward optimize it, and transition it to live trading. Critically, he also explains the ways backtests fail — overfitting, look-ahead bias, selection bias — making students intelligent skeptics of their own systems. The most practical backtesting-to-execution book in the library. Accessible without requiring programming.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Evaluation and Optimization of Trading Strategies — Robert Pardo depth — The academic-practitioner deep dive into walk-forward optimization and out-of-sample testing. More rigorous than Davey's practitioner account — covers statistical significance of backtests, objective function selection, and the robustness testing that separates real edges from curve-fits.
  • Quantitative Trading Systems — Howard Bandy depth — Bandy's systematic approach to building and validating systems covers regime detection, Monte Carlo simulation, and position sizing under uncertainty. Harder than Davey but covers the methodology gaps that lead traders to over-trust their backtests.
  • Design, Testing, and Optimization of Trading Systems — Robert Pardo execution — An earlier, more concise Pardo work covering the same territory. If students find the full Evaluation and Optimization too heavy, this earlier volume is an accessible stepping stone to rigorous backtesting methodology.

Month 5 — Algo Analysis (Conceptual)
M5
Inside the Black Box: The Simple Truth About Quantitative Trading
Rishi K. Narang
Why this book: Demystifies quantitative trading without a single equation. Narang explains how quant funds actually work — alpha generation, risk models, execution systems, portfolio construction — in plain language. After Davey's hands-on approach, this provides the conceptual map of the professional algorithmic landscape. Students understand what they're building toward even before they can code it. Excellent preparation for Level 4's cross-asset systematic work.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • A Man For All Markets — Edward O. Thorp breadth — Thorp invented card-counting and then beat Wall Street using quantitative methods before quant trading existed as a profession. One of the most inspiring stories in the library; gives students a historical anchor for quantitative edge-finding and validates the systematic approach emotionally.
  • The Quants — Scott Patterson breadth — Narrative history of how quantitative traders came to dominate Wall Street. Provides the industry context for Narang's conceptual framework — who built these black boxes and what happened when they all failed simultaneously in 2007.
  • Algorithmic Trading and DMA — Barry Johnson depth — The most technically complete book on algorithmic execution — order types, market microstructure, smart order routing, and DMA mechanics. For students who want to understand not just strategy but how their orders interact with the actual market.

Month 6 — Quantitative Research Synthesis
M6
Quantitative Trading: How to Build Your Own Algorithmic Trading Business
Ernest P. Chan
Why this book: The Level 3 capstone. Chan bridges research process and live execution — how to source ideas, build and test a quantitative strategy, manage execution and transaction costs, and run a systematic trading operation. Light programming context (MATLAB/Excel examples) but fully readable without coding background. Connects the "research to rules" pipeline completed across the prior 5 months into a cohesive professional framework.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • New Trading Systems and Methods — Perry Kaufman depth — The most comprehensive systematic trading methodology reference in the library — covers hundreds of systems, indicators, and approaches with performance statistics. A lifetime reference for students entering systematic research.
  • Successful Algorithmic Trading (QSTrader/Michael Halls-Moore) execution — Bridges the conceptual level of Chan's book to actual Python implementation. For students ready to move from Excel/MATLAB to code-based backtesting — the most practical execution pathway from Chan's framework.
  • What Works on Wall Street — James O'Shaughnessy depth — Largest-scale quantitative study of stock market strategies using decades of data. Provides empirical grounding for the kinds of fundamental and technical factors students may build into their first systematic strategies.

Month 1 — FX Foundations
M1
Day Trading and Swing Trading the Currency Market
Kathy Lien
Why this book: The most comprehensive and practical FX trading book available — covers technical strategies, fundamental drivers (central bank policy, economic releases, carry trade), currency-specific behavior, and tactical trade setups. Lien writes as a practitioner, not a theorist. Best single volume for a trader entering FX markets. Covers all major pairs with specific tactical depth.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • The Little Book of Currency Trading — Kathy Lien execution — Lien's condensed, accessible companion. Useful as a pre-reading primer if students find the full book heavy on first pass, or as a structured review of core concepts at month-end before the exam.
  • Currency Wars — James Rickards breadth — How central bank currency interventions and devaluations shape global FX dynamics. Adds the geopolitical and monetary policy layer missing from Lien's tactical focus — important context for fundamental FX analysis.
  • Forex on Five Hours a Week — Raghee Horner execution — A structured, part-time trading system for FX using the 34 EMA Wave. Very concrete and tactical — good for students who want a simple, rules-based FX execution framework alongside Lien's broader education.

Month 2 — FX Institutional Perspective
M2
Beat the Forex Dealer
Agustín Silvani
Why this book: The only book that explains how FX dealers and banks actually operate — stop hunting, spread manipulation, liquidity dynamics — and how retail traders can navigate against it. Fills a critical blind spot left by Lien's technical approach. After this, students understand who is on the other side of their trades and how to structure entries to avoid institutional disadvantages.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Forex Price Action Scalping — Bob Volman execution — Hyper-focused on short-term price action in FX. Volman's approach is meticulous and rule-bound — ideal for students who want an execution framework built entirely on price without relying on indicators. Demanding but formative for execution discipline.
  • Naked Forex — Alex Nekritin & Walter Peters execution — Price-action-only approach to FX trading using no indicators. Covers big-picture analysis, entry triggers, and trade management. Good for students who want a stripped-down counter-approach to Lien's indicator-based methods.
  • Forex Wave Theory — James Bickford depth — Applies Elliott Wave and cycle analysis to FX markets specifically. For students interested in wave-based analysis as a structured method beyond standard technical patterns.

Month 3 — Options Foundations
M3
Getting Started in Options
Michael C. Thomsett
Why this book: The gentlest entry point into options — puts, calls, basic strategies (covered calls, protective puts, spreads), terminology, and risk profiles — without options pricing theory yet. Sets the vocabulary and intuition before the heavier Natenberg and McMillan books. Students should finish this month able to explain any basic option strategy to a peer.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Understanding Options — Michael Sincere breadth — A parallel accessible intro covering the same foundational territory. Useful as a second exposure with slightly different examples and framing — some students absorb options mechanics better with two explanations of the same concepts.
  • Think Like an Option Trader — Michael Hanania Benklifa breadth — Argues for shifting from stock-market thinking (directional) to options-market thinking (volatility and probability). Builds the mental reframe that makes options education stick — essential before diving into strategy mechanics.
  • Trading Options For Dummies — Joe Duarte execution — The most practically structured options intro for execution — walks through opening a brokerage account, placing trades, managing positions. For students who want to move from concept to live paper-trading during the month.

Month 4 — Options Strategy (Comprehensive)
M4
Options as a Strategic Investment
Lawrence G. McMillan
Why this book: The professional options reference — exhaustive coverage of every major strategy with risk/reward profiles, Greeks exposure, and tactical adjustments. At 1,000+ pages it is not read cover-to-cover; assign the core strategy chapters (Chs. 1–10, 15, 20) and treat the rest as a lifelong reference. After Thomsett's foundation, this is the book practitioners use.
Instructor flag: This is dense and long. Assign specific chapters, not the full book. Focus: buying and selling puts/calls, spreads, straddles, and the Greeks chapter. Omit exotic structures until Level 5.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • The Bible of Options Strategies — Guy Cohen execution — Every options strategy presented in a standardized format: description, sentiment, profit/loss profile, breakevens, Greeks exposure. Pure execution reference — complements McMillan's explanatory prose with a visual strategy-by-strategy lookup format.
  • The Option Trader's Hedge Fund — Dennis Chen & Mark Sebastian execution — Focuses on systematic income generation through options selling (covered calls, cash-secured puts, credit spreads). For students oriented toward income-producing option strategies rather than speculative directional plays.
  • Trading Options Greeks — Dan Passarelli depth — Dedicated book on the Greeks — not just what they are, but how to actively trade them. Delta hedging, vega positioning, theta decay management. Bridges McMillan's strategy coverage with Natenberg's pricing theory, making Month 5 more approachable.

Month 5 — Options Pricing & Volatility
M5
Option Volatility and Pricing
Sheldon Natenberg
Why this book: The text that options market makers study. Covers theoretical pricing, the Greeks (delta, gamma, vega, theta, rho) in full practical depth, volatility trading strategies, and risk management. After two months of strategy, students need to understand why options are priced the way they are and how volatility is the true underlying variable being traded. This permanently upgrades options intuition.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Option Trading: Pricing and Volatility Strategies and Techniques — Euan Sinclair depth — Natenberg for practitioners who want the next level. Covers volatility forecasting models, skew, term structure, and stat-arb strategies around options pricing. Bridges Natenberg's theoretical framework with active vol-trading practice.
  • The Volatility Edge in Options Trading — Jeff Augen execution — Focuses on exploiting structural volatility patterns — pre-earnings, expiration anomalies, event-driven pricing errors. Very execution-oriented; shows students where and when mispricing occurs in practice.
  • Trading VIX Derivatives — Russell Rhoads breadth — Covers VIX futures, options, and ETPs as instruments in their own right. Extends Natenberg's volatility framework to the volatility asset class itself — important as volatility products have become a distinct tradeable market.

Month 7 — Derivatives (Comprehensive)
M7
Options, Futures and Other Derivatives
John C. Hull
Why this book: The academic standard for derivatives — used in every finance MBA program and professional certification. Covers futures pricing, binomial trees, Black-Scholes, swaps, credit derivatives, exotic options. Not every section is accessible to every student, but the conceptual rigor it provides on pricing and risk management is unmatched. Assign Chs. 1–6 (futures fundamentals) and Chs. 10–12 (basic options pricing) as core; rest as optional depth.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Derivatives Demystified — Andrew Chisholm breadth — Plain-language walkthrough of forwards, futures, swaps, and options for non-quants. The accessible companion for students struggling with Hull's mathematical sections — covers the same instruments conceptually without the algebra.
  • Commodities and Commodity Derivatives — Hélyette Geman depth — Specialized treatment of commodity derivatives pricing — energy, metals, agriculture — with Python-based analytics. For students with a quantitative background who want commodity-specific derivative modelling beyond Hull's generic framework.
  • Derivatives Analytics with Python — Yves Hilpisch execution — Implements Hull's derivative pricing models in Python. Bridges the conceptual/mathematical content of Hull to executable code — the natural next step for students who want to actually build derivative pricing tools.

Month 9 — Crypto & Digital Assets
M9
The Bitcoin Standard
Saifedean Ammous
Why this book: The strongest first-principles argument for understanding what Bitcoin and crypto actually represent economically — monetary theory, sound money, the history of monetary debasement, and what decentralized currency changes about the financial system. Students who only read crypto trading tactics miss the structural shift crypto represents. This provides the "why does this asset class exist?" foundation that makes crypto more than speculative noise.

  • The Internet of Money — Andreas Antonopoulos breadth — Collected talks on why Bitcoin and blockchain matter beyond price speculation — monetary freedom, banking the unbanked, programmable money. Broadens students' frame beyond price-trading to the structural disruption crypto represents.
  • Cryptocurrency: Ultimate Beginners Guide — Matthew Connor execution — Practical coverage of the broader crypto market ecosystem — Bitcoin, Ethereum, altcoins, exchanges, wallets, and market dynamics. For students who want to understand the actual crypto trading landscape rather than just Bitcoin's economic theory.

Month 10 — Intermarket Analysis
M10
Trading with Intermarket Analysis
John J. Murphy
Why this book: How the four major asset classes — stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies — interact and lead each other. After 9 months studying each individually, students need to see the unified picture. Murphy shows how commodity moves predict inflation, how inflation affects bond prices, how bond yields affect equities, and how FX flows tie everything together. Using ETFs as the practical vehicle makes this immediately actionable.
📚 Supplementary — if time allows
  • Intermarket Trading Strategies — Markos Katsanos execution — Quantifies Murphy's intermarket relationships into backtested, rule-based trading systems. Bridges the conceptual intermarket framework with actual executable signals — the natural "now trade it" companion to Murphy's theory.
  • Global Macro Trading — Greg Gliner depth — Practitioner-level macro trading — how professional managers build macro views, size positions across asset classes, and manage drawdowns. Elevates the intermarket conceptual framework to a professional portfolio management practice.
  • Trading and Exchanges — Larry Harris depth — Market microstructure across all asset classes — how prices are actually formed, who the market participants are, and how information flows between markets. Provides the structural foundation under Murphy's macro relationships.

Month 11 — Systematic Multi-Asset Trading
M11
Following the Trend
Andreas F. Clenow
Why this book: The most rigorous and accessible book on systematic trend following across all futures markets — equities, FX, commodities, rates. Clenow is a practitioner who publishes his full strategy code. After 10 months studying individual instruments, this shows how to systematically combine them under one strategy framework. Evidence-based, rules-based, and written at a level that rewards both beginners and advanced traders.

  • Trend Following — Michael Covel breadth — The philosophical and historical case for trend following as a trading approach — profiles of the Turtle traders, Dunn, Henry, Millburn. 📚 Supplementary

  • The Fourth Turning — Strauss & Howe; generational cycle theory; a macro framework that complements Dalio's debt-cycle lens breadth

The definitive historical study of financial crises across five centuries. Teaches pattern recognition in bubble/crash dynamics.

Soros's theory of reflexivity — how market participants' beliefs alter the fundamentals they reflect. One of the most original ideas in finance.
